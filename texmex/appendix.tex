\chapter{Block Labeling Method}
\label{app:block_label_method}
The higher-dimensional energy sectors can contain several independent
subspaces associated with different combinations of subsystem energy levels.
This is especially clear in the non-interacting and weakly interacting
configurations, where a \(24\)-dimensional sector commonly decomposes into
three \(8\)-dimensional blocks and a \(72\)-dimensional sector into nine such
blocks. The \(24\)-dimensional projected-operator example is illustrated in
\cref{fig:24x24maj}. At stronger interaction strengths, an energy
sector may intersect only part of one of these blocks, so neither the sector
dimension nor every labelled block is necessarily a multiple of eight.

The blocks are identified from the energy levels of the three individual
subsystems. For a single three-dot subsystem, the four even-parity
eigenstates are paired with the four odd-parity eigenstates by nearest energy.
For pair \(\ell\), define the projector
\begin{equation}
  Q_{\ell}
  =
  \ket{e_{\ell}}\bra{e_{\ell}}
  +
  \ket{o_{\ell}}\bra{o_{\ell}},
  \qquad
  \ell=0,1,2,3.
\end{equation}
The single-subsystem label operator is then
\begin{equation}
  L_{\mathrm{single}}
  =
  \sum_{\ell=0}^{3} \ell Q_{\ell}.
\end{equation}
Its eigenvalue records which even--odd energy pair a state belongs to without
distinguishing the two parity states within that pair.

To label the three-subsystem product states, \(L_{\mathrm{single}}\) is
embedded separately on subsystems \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\):
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    L_A &= L_{\mathrm{single}}\otimes I\otimes I,\\
    L_B &= I\otimes L_{\mathrm{single}}\otimes I,\\
    L_C &= I\otimes I\otimes L_{\mathrm{single}}.
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
These operators are combined using a positional label,
\begin{equation}
  L_{\mathrm{full}}
  =
  L_A + B L_B + B^2 L_C,
  \qquad B=5.
\end{equation}
Because \(B\) is larger than the greatest single-subsystem label, each
eigenvalue
\begin{equation}
  \lambda(\ell_A,\ell_B,\ell_C)
  =
  \ell_A+B\ell_B+B^2\ell_C
\end{equation}
uniquely identifies the tuple \((\ell_A,\ell_B,\ell_C)\). In the full Hilbert
space, each tuple spans the eight product states obtained by independently
choosing even or odd parity in the three subsystems.

Let \(V_g\) contain an orthonormal basis for energy sector \(g\). The full
label operator is projected into this sector as
\begin{equation}
  L_g
  =
  V_g^{\dagger}L_{\mathrm{full}}V_g.
\end{equation}
Diagonalizing \(L_g\),
\begin{equation}
  L_g w_i=\lambda_i w_i,
\end{equation}
groups the sector basis vectors by their encoded subsystem labels. Vectors
with the same eigenvalue are collected in a block basis \(W_r\). For an
operator \(O_g\) already projected into sector \(g\), its restriction to block
\(r\) is
\begin{equation}
  O_g^{(r)}
  =
  W_r^{\dagger}O_g W_r.
\end{equation}

Within each labelled block, the projected local endpoint operator is fitted
independently to the two corresponding energy-level Majorana components, as
defined in \cref{eq:method_block_matched_majorana}. The fitted blocks are then
reassembled into an operator on the complete sector. The label construction
only identifies the independent blocks; it does not impose the Majorana
algebra, which is checked separately after projection.
In well defined cases, the block-matched operator is simply a signed version of the energy-level Majorana operator, but for stronger interacting systems, the block-matched operator can deviate. Numerical results for the block-matched coefficients are reported in \cref{app:matched_operator_coefficients}.

\chapter{Block-Matched Coefficients}
\label{app:matched_operator_coefficients}

For each selected energy sector, the projected local endpoint operators were
fitted independently within the labelled blocks to the two corresponding
energy-level Majorana components. The fitted first-component coefficients
vanish at the saved precision, while the second-component coefficients are
close to either \(+1\) or \(-1\) in most blocks. The blockwise coefficients and
their relative signs are reported in \cref{tab:matched_operator_coefficients}.

For each labelled block \(r\), the projected local endpoint operator of
subsystem \(S=B,C\) is restricted to the block and fitted to the two
energy-level Majorana components,
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_{S,\mathrm{loc}}^{(r)}
  \approx
  a_{S,r}\gamma_{S1}^{(r)}
  +
  b_{S,r}\gamma_{S2}^{(r)} .
\end{equation}
The table reports the coefficients \(b_{S,r}\), since the fitted
first-component coefficients \(a_{S,r}\) vanish at the saved precision.

The ideal expectation is therefore \(a_{S,r}\approx0\) and
\(b_{S,r}\approx\pm1\). The sign is not by itself an error, but fixes the
orientation of the fitted local operator relative to the chosen energy-level
Majorana convention. The magnitude tests whether the fitted operator is still a
properly normalized Majorana within the block. If the two energy-level
components satisfy
\((\gamma_{S1}^{(r)})^2=(\gamma_{S2}^{(r)})^2=P_r\) and
\(\{\gamma_{S1}^{(r)},\gamma_{S2}^{(r)}\}=0\), then
\begin{equation}
  \left(\Gamma_{S,\mathrm{fit}}^{(r)}\right)^2
  \approx
  \left(a_{S,r}^2+b_{S,r}^2\right)P_r .
\end{equation}
Thus, in the present case where \(a_{S,r}\approx0\), deviations of
\(|b_{S,r}|\) from one measure how far the projected local operator is from
squaring to the block projector.

For the braid, the two fitted endpoint operators enter through the product
\begin{equation}
  b_{B,r}b_{C,r}
  =
  s_r\alpha_r,
  \qquad
  s_r=\operatorname{sgn}(b_{B,r}b_{C,r}),
  \qquad
  \alpha_r=|b_{B,r}b_{C,r}|.
\end{equation}
Here \(s_r\) gives the exchange orientation in block \(r\), while
\(\alpha_r\) controls the effective rotation strength. Mixed signs therefore
mean that different blocks follow opposite exchange orientations, while
magnitudes different from one indicate a non-ideal Majorana normalization or
rotation angle.
